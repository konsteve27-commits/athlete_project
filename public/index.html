Early Life

Sha'Carri Richardson was born in Dallas, Texas, and raised by her supportive grandmother, Betty Harp, and aunt, Shayaria, after a difficult relationship with her biological parents; she became a track star at Carter High School, winning national titles in middle school before dominating in high school and briefly at LSU, setting the stage for her professional career despite early family hardships and self-doubt.

Professional Career

Who is Sha'Carri Richardson? All About the Fastest Woman in ...Sha'Carri Richardson's early professional career exploded in 2019 after a sensational freshman year at LSU, where she set NCAA records in the 100m (10.75s) and 200m, earning The Bowerman award before turning pro, stunning the track world with her speed, distinct style, and immediately running world-class times, establishing herself as a dominant force and cultural icon in sprinting.

Full Biography

2021: Trials and Tribulations

In April 2021, Richardson clocked 10.72 seconds, becoming the sixth-fastest woman ever at the time. She won the 100m at the U.S. Olympic Trials with 10.86s. However, her victory was invalidated following a positive test for cannabis, which she used to cope with the loss of her biological mother. This led to a one-month suspension, causing her to miss the Tokyo Olympics.

2023: World Championship Dominance

The year 2023 marked Richardson's ultimate comeback. In July, she became the US national champion with 10.82s. In August, at the World Athletics Championships in Budapest, she won the 100m Gold from Lane 9, setting a championship record of 10.65s. She also anchored Team USA to Gold in the 4x100m relay (41.03s) and secured a Bronze in the 200m.

2024: Olympic Glory in Paris

Richardson defended her US 100m title with a world-leading 10.71s. At the Paris Olympics, she secured the Silver medal in the 100m. Her standout moment came in the 4x100m relay where, as the anchor, she propelled the U.S. from third to first place, clinching her first Olympic Gold medal and creating an iconic "stare-down" moment at the finish line.

2025: Continuing the Legacy

In 2025, Richardson continued her international dominance. At the World Athletics Championships in Tokyo, she remained a key pillar for Team USA. She helped secure another Gold medal in the women's 4x100m relay with a time of 41.75 seconds, maintaining her status as one of the fastest and most influential relay anchors in history.
